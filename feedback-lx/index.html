Why Feedback Feels Hard

Understanding the psychology helps us navigate the discomfort.

Even when we want to give or receive feedback, something can short-circuit the conversation. Research identifies three "triggers" that hijack our response:

🎯
Truth Triggers

Truth Triggers
The feedback feels wrong, unfair, or unhelpful. We focus on what’s inaccurate rather than what might be useful.
👥
Relationship Triggers

Relationship Triggers
We discount the feedback based on who’s giving it. “Who are they to tell me this?”
🪞
Identity Triggers

Identity Triggers
The feedback threatens our sense of self. It feels like it’s about who we are, not what we did.

On the giving side, we hesitate because we:

  • Worry about damaging the relationship
  • Fear the other person will get defensive
  • Question whether it’s “our place” to say something
  • Aren’t sure how to frame it constructively

The good news: These are skills you can build. Let’s start with understanding what feedback actually is.

📚 Three Types

Not All Feedback Is the Same

Understanding the type helps you deliver it well – and receive it openly.

💛
Appreciation
"I see you. You matter."
Recognition that motivates and builds connection. Everyone needs it.
💚
Coaching
"Here's how you can improve."
Guidance aimed at helping someone learn, grow, or change.
💙
Evaluation
"Here's where you stand."
Rating or ranking against expectations. Typically a manager responsibility.

For Peer Feedback, Focus on Appreciation + Coaching

As peers, most of our feedback falls into appreciation (reinforcing what's working) and coaching (helping each other improve). We'll focus primarily on coaching – the "constructive" feedback that often feels hardest but creates the most growth.

🎯 The Framework

SBI: The Feedback Framework

A simple structure that keeps feedback specific, fair, and actionable.

S
Situation
When and where did this happen? Be specific about the context.
"In Monday's project sync with the design team..."
B
Behavior
What did you directly observe? Stick to facts, not interpretations.
"You interrupted twice while Sam was explaining the timeline..."
I
Impact
How did it affect you, the team, or the outcome?
"...which made it harder for us to understand the full picture before jumping into solutions."

Why SBI Works

It separates facts from feelings. By anchoring in a specific situation and observable behavior, you avoid generalizations ("You always...") and character judgments ("You're disorganized"). This makes feedback easier to hear and act on.

💡
For Live Conversations: IDEA

SBI is excellent for written feedback and structured documentation like Workday. But in a live conversation, you need more: why you're bringing it up, a two-way exchange, and a path forward. That's where IDEA comes in.

I
Intent
D
Describe
E
Exchange
A
Action
Same feedback, two frameworks – tap to compare
SBI (Written)
IDEA (In Person)
A colleague interrupted twice in a team meeting:
S "In Monday's project sync with the design team..."
B "You interrupted Sam twice while they were explaining the timeline."
I "It made it harder for us to understand the full picture before jumping into solutions."
Best for: Workday feedback, Slack follow-ups, any written channel where you want to be precise and concise.
Same situation, but you're sitting down with them in a 1:1:
I "I wanted to bring this up because I really value our working relationship and I think this will help us collaborate better."
D "In Monday's sync, you jumped in twice while Sam was explaining the timeline. It meant we missed some context before moving to solutions."
E "I'm curious how you saw it – did you feel the same way, or was there something I missed?"
A "What if next time we let each person finish their update before we jump into problem-solving?"
Why this works live: Leading with Intent lowers defensiveness. Describe keeps it factual. Exchange makes it a conversation, not a lecture – you invite their perspective before proposing next steps. Action makes it forward-looking. The other person leaves knowing why you care, feeling heard, and clear on what to try.

Rule of thumb: Writing it down? Use SBI. Saying it live? Open with IDEA – then you can flex to SBI for the middle if you want.

⭐ The Unlock

"One Thing Better"

Chime's approach to making feedback feel manageable.

💡
The Key Question
"What's one thing you could have done better?"

This simple prompt transforms feedback from overwhelming to actionable. It works whether you're giving feedback or asking for it.

✅
Bite-sized: One thing is manageable. It doesn't overwhelm.
✅
Non-threatening: "Better" implies growth, not failure.
✅
Universal: Everyone can improve one thing – it normalizes feedback.
✅
Actionable: Forces specificity instead of vague concerns.

How to Use It

When giving feedback: After sharing SBI, add: "One thing to try next time – what if you [specific suggestion]?"

When asking for feedback: "Hey, I just wrapped up [project/meeting/presentation]. What's one thing I could have done better?"

In real-time: Use it after meetings, presentations, project milestones – any moment where quick feedback can help.

The Other Side: Receiving Well

Giving feedback is only half the equation. How we receive it matters just as much.

The Emotional Journey: SARA

When we receive critical feedback, we often move through predictable stages. Knowing this helps us navigate the emotions.

S
Surprise
"Hmm, really?"
A
Awareness
"They don't get it."
R
Resistance
"Yeah, but..."
A
Acceptance
"OK, I can see that."

Click each stage to learn more

Key insight: How quickly we move from surprise to acceptance depends on context, delivery, and our own mindset. The goal isn't to skip the emotions – it's to not get stuck in them.

💡 Remember
Listening to understand ≠ agreeing. You can hear someone out, ask questions, and still decide what (if anything) to change. The goal is to fully understand before you respond.

📱 Delivery Methods

In-Person vs. Feedback in Workday

Different channels, different best practices.

💬
In-Person / Live
💼
Feedback in Workday
⏰
Best served fresh: Give feedback within 24-48 hours when it's still actionable.
🚪
Private for constructive: Have coaching conversations in 1:1 settings, not in front of others.
🙋
Ask permission first: "Got 5 minutes for some feedback?" That small "yes" lowers defensiveness.
👂
Stay for the reaction: Don't drop feedback and run. Be there to listen and discuss.
🔁
Follow up later: "How's it going with the thing we discussed?" shows you care.
📝
Try the recommended 3-Question Template: (1) How did you work together? (2) What did they do well? (3) One thing better?
🎯
Be specific: Written feedback needs even more context since you can't read body language. Use SBI.
⚖️
Balance it: Include both reinforcing (what went well) and constructive (one thing better) feedback.
💬
Offer to discuss: End with "Happy to talk through this live if helpful."
📅
Don't wait for reviews: Feedback in Workday is for ongoing feedback, not just annual cycles.

Feedback Across Boundaries

Two scenarios: giving feedback across teams, and giving feedback upward.

Scenario 1 of 2: Cross-Functional
The Situation

You've been collaborating with Alex from the Data team on a dashboard project. In yesterday's stakeholder meeting, Alex presented the data in a way that made your team's work look ineffective – they focused only on the problems without acknowledging the improvements you'd made.

What's your move?
Option A
"Hey Alex, in yesterday's meeting the dashboard framing focused on gaps without mentioning the 20% improvement from last quarter, which made our work look ineffective. Could we align on framing before stakeholder meetings?"
Option B
"I'm going to raise this with Alex's manager. The way they presented the data wasn’t balanced – it made our whole team look bad. If Alex won't hear it from me, their manager needs to know what happened."
Option C
"Alex, I wanted to bring something up from yesterday. The way the data was framed in that meeting didn't sit right with me – it felt like our team's contributions were overlooked. Can we talk about it?"
Scenario 2 of 2: Upward Feedback
The Situation

Your manager has been canceling your 1:1s for three weeks in a row. Each time, they reschedule but then cancel again. You have questions about your project direction and feel stuck without their input.

How do you raise this?
Option A
"Hey, I know things have been packed lately. Our last three 1:1s got canceled, and I have some project questions building up that are slowing my decisions. Could we protect even 15 minutes this week? I want to make sure I'm heading the right direction."
Option B
"I guess our 1:1s aren't a priority anymore. I've been figuring things out on my own, which is fine, but don't be surprised if something goes sideways."
Option C
"No worries about the cancellations! I know you're busy. I'll just ping you on Slack if anything urgent comes up."
