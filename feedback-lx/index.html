The Psychology

The Feedback Paradox

Why something we say we want can feel so threatening.

Before we get into frameworks and practice, it helps to understand something fundamental about how we're wired. Researchers Douglas Stone and Sheila Heen at Harvard identified a tension that sits at the core of every feedback conversation:

🌱
The Need to Grow

"I want to learn, improve, and become better at what I do. Tell me what I'm missing."

vs
🛡
The Need to Be Accepted

"I want to be respected and valued for who I am right now. Don't tell me I'm not enough."

Both of these needs are real, legitimate, and present in every one of us. We genuinely want to grow. And we genuinely want to feel accepted. The problem is that feedback activates both at the same time.

When someone says, "Here's something you could do differently," part of you hears a growth opportunity. But another part hears: you're not OK as you are. That's not a character flaw – it's human psychology.

Psychologist William Swann's self-verification research (1983) revealed something counterintuitive: people don't just want positive feedback. They want consistent feedback – information that matches how they already see themselves. When feedback confirms our self-image, it feels safe and coherent. When it challenges our self-image, even positively, it creates dissonance.

This means feedback can feel threatening even when it's accurate, kind, and well-intentioned. It's not the feedback that's the problem – it's that the feedback is asking us to update our picture of who we are, and our brains resist that instinctively.

🌱

🛡

The point isn't to silence either voice. It's to recognize both are in the room every time feedback happens – for you and for the person you're giving feedback to. That awareness is what makes the difference between feedback that lands and feedback that backfires.

Three Types

Not All Feedback Is the Same

Understanding the type helps you deliver it well – and receive it openly.

💛
Appreciation
"I see you. You matter."
Recognition that motivates and builds connection. Everyone needs it.
💚
Coaching
"Here's how you can improve."
Guidance aimed at helping someone learn, grow, or change.
💙
Evaluation
"Here's where you stand."
Rating or ranking against expectations. Usually from managers.

For Peer Feedback, Focus on Appreciation + Coaching

As peers, most of our feedback falls into appreciation (reinforcing what's working) and coaching (helping each other improve). We'll focus primarily on coaching – the "constructive" feedback that often feels hardest but creates the most growth.

The Framework

SBI: A Framework for Clear Feedback

A simple structure that keeps feedback specific, fair, and actionable.

S
Situation
When and where did this happen? Be specific about the context.
"In Monday's project sync with the design team..."
B
Behavior
What did you directly observe? Stick to facts, not interpretations.
"You interrupted twice while Sam was explaining the timeline..."
I
Impact
How did it affect you, the team, or the outcome?
"...which made it harder for us to understand the full picture before jumping into solutions."

Why SBI Works

It separates facts from feelings. By anchoring in a specific situation and observable behavior, you avoid generalizations ("You always...") and character judgments ("You're disorganized"). This makes feedback easier to hear and act on.

💡
Another Way to Frame It: IDEA
I
Intention
D
Data
E
Effect
A
Action

IDEA adds Intention (why you're sharing this) and Action (what to do differently). SBI is the standard model used at Chime, but IDEA can supplement your thinking.

The Unlock

"One Thing Better"

An approach to making feedback feel manageable.

💡
The Key Question
"What's one thing you could have done better?"

This simple prompt transforms feedback from overwhelming to actionable. It works whether you're giving feedback or asking for it.

✓
Bite-sized: One thing is manageable. It doesn't overwhelm.
✓
Non-threatening: "Better" implies growth, not failure.
✓
Universal: Everyone can improve one thing – it normalizes feedback.
✓
Actionable: Forces specificity instead of vague concerns.

How to Use It

When giving feedback: After sharing SBI, add: "One thing to try next time – what if you [specific suggestion]?"

When asking for feedback: "Hey, I just wrapped up [project/meeting/presentation]. What's one thing I could have done better?"

In real-time: Use it after meetings, presentations, project milestones – any moment where quick feedback can help.

Key insight: How quickly we move from shock to acceptance depends on context, delivery, and our own mindset. The goal isn't to skip the emotions – it's to not get stuck in them.

Remember

What to Avoid

❌ Defending immediately – Let them finish before responding.

❌ Dismissing – "That's just your opinion" shuts down the conversation.

❌ Counter-attacking – "Well, you also..." derails into conflict.

Delivery Methods

In-Person vs. Feedback in Workday

Different channels, different best practices.

💬
In-Person / Live
💼
Feedback in Workday
⏰
Best served fresh: Give feedback within 24–48 hours when it's still actionable.
🚪
Private for constructive: Have coaching conversations in 1:1 settings, not in front of others.
🙋
Ask permission first: "Got 5 minutes for some feedback?" That small "yes" lowers defensiveness.
👂
Stay for the reaction: Don't drop feedback and run. Be there to listen and discuss.
🔁
Follow up later: "How's it going with the thing we discussed?" shows you care.
📝
Use Chime's 3-Question Template: (1) How did you work together? (2) What did they do well? (3) One thing better?
🎯
Be specific: Written feedback needs even more context since you can't read body language. Use SBI.
⚖
Balance it: Include both reinforcing (what went well) and constructive (one thing better) feedback.
💬
Offer to discuss: End with "Happy to talk through this live if helpful."
📅
Don't wait for reviews: Feedback in Workday is for ongoing feedback, not just annual cycles.
