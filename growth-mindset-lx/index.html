Core Concepts

Two Mindsets, Two Worlds

Psychologist Carol Dweck spent decades studying how people think about their own abilities. She identified two distinct mindsets:

🔒

Fixed Mindset

Believes abilities are static traits. You either have them or you don't. Effort is a sign you're not naturally good. Feedback feels like judgment.

🌱

Growth Mindset

Believes abilities can be developed. Challenges are opportunities. Feedback is information. Difficulty is a normal part of learning.

Key insight: Most people have both mindsets - it depends on the situation.

The Science

Your Brain Can Change

This isn't just motivational talk. It's neuroscience.

🧠
Your brain is plastic - it can change and reorganize itself throughout your life. When you practice a skill, the neural pathways get stronger. Your brain literally rewires itself.

Every time you practice something difficult, your brain strengthens the connections between neurons involved in that task. With repetition, those connections become faster and more efficient - what neuroscientists call myelination.

This means the feeling of difficulty isn't a sign you can't do it - it's literally your brain building new capacity. The discomfort of learning IS the growth happening in real time.

"In a growth mindset, challenges are exciting rather than threatening. So rather than thinking, oh, I'm going to reveal my weaknesses, you say, wow, here's a chance to grow."
- Carol Dweck, Mindset: The New Psychology of Success

Practical Tools

Three Techniques to Shift Your Mindset

1

The Power of "Yet"

When you catch yourself thinking "I can't do this," add one word: yet.

"I can't do data analysis" → "I can't do data analysis yet"
Try it: Write something you "can't" do and add "yet"
2

Growth Questions

Ask questions that redirect from protection to learning.

  • "What can I learn from this?"
  • "What would I try if I weren't afraid of failing?"
  • "Who could help me figure this out?"
  • "What's the smallest next step I could take?"
3

Reattribution

Notice how you explain difficulty to yourself:

"This is hard because I'm bad at it" → "This is hard because it's new"

J
Jordan - Your manager
In your 1:1: "There's a new cross-functional project kicking off - data migration with Engineering and Compliance. It's high-visibility. I think you'd be great for it, but it's definitely a stretch. What do you think?"

Your stomach tightens. You've never led anything with Compliance before. What if you fall short in front of everyone?

Option C
"Sure, I can do that - sounds like a great opportunity. When do we start? I'll figure out the Compliance piece as I go, no need to overthink it."
